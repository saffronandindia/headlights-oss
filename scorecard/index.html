How to read this

Each product is scored 0–3 on seven dimensions, totalling out of 21. Scores come from public documentation only. We do not infer, speculate, or grade on private demos. Where a vendor's marketing makes a claim that public technical documentation does not substantiate, the dimension is marked U rather than zeroed — the vendor is invited to publish docs, after which we re-score.

Full rubric: specs/scorecard-rubric.md. Per-vendor sources: specs/scorecard-research.md.

The seven dimensions:
A. Tamper evidence — Is the log cryptographically chained, or is it merely "trust us, we won't modify it"?
B. Per-record signatures — Can a specific log entry be proven to have been issued by the vendor or agent?
C. Retention — How long are records kept? Configurable how far?
D. Replayability — Does the log capture what was asked and what was produced, or just metadata?
E. External anchoring — Are log hashes committed to a medium outside the vendor's control?
F. Independent verifiability — Can a customer (or their auditor) confirm integrity without trusting the vendor?
G. Standards alignment — Does the product align with any open AI-specific audit standard (IETF AAT, ISO 42001)?

What we learned

No major AI vendor publicly claims cryptographic tamper-evidence on their audit logs. All seven describe their logs as "immutable" or "append-only," but none publicly document hash chaining, Merkle structures, or per-entry signatures on the contents of the audit log. Every claim of immutability reduces to "trust the vendor."

External anchoring (blockchain, third-party notary, independent WORM) is universally absent. Not one of the seven vendors publicly anchors log hashes outside their own infrastructure. The technical lift (RFC 3161 trusted timestamping) is modest — this is a posture choice, not a capability gap.

Replayability splits the field cleanly. Anthropic, OpenAI Enterprise, and Salesforce capture actual prompt and response content. Microsoft 365 Copilot, Google Gemini, and GitHub Copilot deliberately strip content from the audit log. The Microsoft and Google design choice directly conflicts with the regulatory direction in EU AI Act Article 12, which requires the ability to reconstruct an agent's decisions.

ISO 42001 is becoming the de facto AI-governance certification. Anthropic and OpenAI both hold it; Microsoft has it for Security Copilot and Azure AI Foundry. No vendor surfaced as referencing the IETF AAT draft — expected, since the draft is two months old, but worth tracking.
